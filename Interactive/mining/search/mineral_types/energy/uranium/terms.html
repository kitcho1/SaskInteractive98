In order to understand this energy mineral and its uses, take a look at these terms associated with uranium. Uranium is a useful mineral and one which is increasingly in demand to help the world meet its energy needs.

  • Radioactivity
  • Radiation
  • Fission

Radioactivity

  • Radioactivity is the process of spontaneous decay.
  • Radioactive atoms are atoms that can disintegrate (or decay) spontaneously.
  • Some atoms (like uranium) undergo spontaneous change because of differing numbers if neutrons in their nucleus.
  • The unstable atoms undergo radioactive disintegration in an attempt to obtain stability. The 'new' atom is called an isotope. Isotope of atoms differ in the number of neutrons in their nucleus form the original element. Radioisotopes are unstable and emit radiation when they change from one isotope to another.
  • The end product of these changes will be stable (non-radioactive).
  • The time it take for one isotope to decay to the next isotope is called the half-life of that element.
  • Uranium has fifteen unstable isotopes, three of which occur naturally; uranium-238 (most common), uranium-235, and uranium-234.
  • Atoms that decay give off ionizing radiation.

Radiation

  • Radiation is the energy emitted by radioactive ions.
  • Radiation is a form of energy that travels through space and gives up all or part of its energy upon contact with matter.
  • Ionizing radiation is given off by atoms undergoing radioactive disintegration. It consist of alpha, beta and gamma radiation.
  • Ionizing radiation can produce biological changes in living matter.
  • Ionizing radiation can also be generated by x-ray tubes.

see also Radiation Safety

Types of Radiation
Background Radiation - radiation which exists naturally around us
  • sun and stars (cosmic radiation)
  • naturally occurring radioactive materials in the Earth's crust such as uranium, potassium, thorium
  • building materials (radium and radon)
  • food and water that we eat and drink
  • our own bodies, due to the radioactive carbon and potassium contained in them
Artificial Radiation - radiation emitted from other than natural sources
  • medical and dental x-rays
  • nuclear reactors
  • cancer therapy treatment
  • smoke detectors
  • exit signs containing tritium

Fission

  • U-235 is easily fissioned (less stable than U-238)
  • The uranium atom nucleus (protons and neutrons) is held together by a binding force.
  • When the binding force of the nucleus is overcome, fission occurs.
  • Fission is the splitting of the nucleus into two parts.
  • Heat and radiation are released.
