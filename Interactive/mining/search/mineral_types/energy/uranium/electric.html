Electricity is a manufactured product that cannot be stored in large amounts. It must be used as soon as it is produced. About 30% of our energy use in the world is to generate electricity.

Uranium is used as fuel for nuclear power reactors, which are used to produce electricity. The uranium fuel is used to create controlled nuclear fission reactions which generate vast quantities of heat. The heat converts water to steam which drives turbines which create electricity.

In addition to nuclear power reactors, electricity can be produced using other fuels as a source of heat. Coal, wood or natural gas are used to convert water to high pressure steam to turn turbines. Electric generating plants, then, differ in the source of the heat.

Generation of Electricity
1. Heat changes the water in the boiler into steam.
2. Steam pressure causes the turbines to rotate.
3. The turning of the turbines rotates the electrical generators.
4. Energy (electricity) is produced and fed into the power lines.

In Saskatchewan, coal has been used as the primary source of fuel for the generation of electricity. In fact, there are relatively few nuclear power reactors in Canada, partly due to the abundance of fossil fuels and the lower capital costs to operate (in comparison to uranium). Uranium, though, provides a huge amount of energy. During nuclear fission, a golf ball-sized piece of uranium can release 2.5 million times more heat than the combustion of an equal weight of carbon in coal or oil. That's a lot of heat! In addition, the release of this heat does not contribute to carbon monoxide or dioxide to our atmosphere. By using nuclear fuel to power electrical generating stations, countries can reduce their dependence on fossil fuels. It also reduces operating costs, although capital costs are higher.


Nuclear Fuel

The type of fuel for nuclear reactors varies depending on the type of reactor. CANDU reactors use uranium dioxide (UO2) while other commercial reactors use enriched uranium fuel (concentration of U-235 is increased).

How is uranium fuel made?

  1. Uranium concentrate is converted into uranium dioxide.
  2. Uranium dioxide is formed into pellets and placed in tubes that are welded shut and bundled together. One bundle 10 centimetres in diameter, 50 centimetres long and weighing 24 kilograms can supply the same amount of electricity as 400 tonnes (about 7 railcars full) of coal or 302 cubic meters (1900 barrels) of oil.
  3. The fuel bundles remain in the reactor about one and a half years.

What is done with the spent uranium fuel?

Once the fuel bundles are used-up, they are removed from the power station and transferred to storage bays filled with water. The water cools the bundles and acts as a shield against radiation emissions. The spent fuel bundles still contain about 98.6% uranium-238 and 0.2% uranium-235 as well as some plutonium and other isotopes. This means that the bundles are still very radioactive.


The Nuclear Reactor

The technology to produce electricity through the use of nuclear reactors was developed during the 1950's. The first commercial nuclear reactor was constructed in Great Britain in 1956. It was discovered that it was possible to use controlled fission (the breaking apart of the nucleus of a uranium atom) to produce heat. This heat was then used to produce the stream that turned the turbines to generate electricity.

Nuclear reactors have been constructed to produce heat and at the same time control radioactivity. Different kinds of reactors have different built-in safety features that are activated in the event of mechanical failure.

Reactor Safety Features
Passive System
  • containment building
  • mechanical design for system shut down; can be overridden by human operators
Automatic System
  • containment building
  • mechanical design for system shut down by reactor itself; human operators have no control over shutdown

Some of these safety features have back-up systems to ensure the greatest possible degree of safety.
The goals of the safety system are:

  • accident prevention
  • minimizing consequences if an accident occurs
  • built-in designs for prevention of effects due to a major accident

Note: Nuclear explosions (bombs) cannot be created in a reactor.

There are a number of different types of nuclear reactors. They all work on the same principle: the fissioning of uranium.


The CANDU Reactor

In Canada, electricity is produced using uranium in the CANDU reactor. This nuclear reactor was designed in Canada during the 1960's. Natural uranium is used for fuel and heavy water is used as the coolant. This means that the heavy water transfers heat from the uranium fuel (the heat being produced by the fissioning uranium) to ordinary water in the steam generator. The steam generator produces the steam that drives the turbine. The turbine drives the generator producing electricity.

A thick concrete containment wall and other safety features are part of the CANDU reactor, making it the safest nuclear reactor available. The concrete enclosure and other features would prevent most of the radioactive material from escaping if an accident occurred. The CANDU is the only reactor that can be refueled without being shut down.
