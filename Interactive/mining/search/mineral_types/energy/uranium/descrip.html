Abundant

  • Uranium is one of the more commonly found heavy elements in the Earth's crust.
  • It is 500 times more abundant than gold and about twice as common as tin.
  • Naturally occurring traces of uranium can be found everywhere - in rocks and soils, rivers and oceans, as well as in food and human tissue.

Background Radiation

  • Because uranium is naturally radioactive and exists virtually everywhere, it contributes to what is called natural background radiation.

Concentration

  • Uranium concentrations vary from substance to substance and place to place.
    • Uranium in phosphate rock, which is used to produce fertilizer, can range as high as 400 ppm (parts per million).
    • Some coal deposits contain uranium concentration levels as high as 1000 ppm.
    • Concentrations in excess of greater than 750 ppm are usually considered ore, or rock economical to mine.

Heavy Metal

  • Uranium has a high density (mass per volume) and the highest atomic weight of all naturally occurring elements.
  • It is as hard a glass.
  • Uranium metal by itself is white and lustrous.
  • Uranium metal takes on a high polish and tarnishes if exposed to air.
  • Metals commonly associated with uranium are nickel, cobalt or silver.

Non-renewable

  • Uranium, like oil and natural gas is a non-renewable resource. To replace uranium, in terms of a human's lifespan, would be impossible because it would take millions of years.

Pitchblende

  • Uranium is usually found in two common mineral forms: pitchblende and uraninite.
  • The uranium ores in Saskatchewan are predominately pitchblende which is a sooty, shiny, black mineral.
  • Uranium occurs in nature in combination with other elements such as oxygen (as in pitchblende U2O3).
